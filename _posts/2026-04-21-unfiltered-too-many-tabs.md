### Welcome to the Chaos. Grab a Chip.

I spent over 40 years living in a room where 50 people were talking at once—half of them strangers, the other half people I love—and they all started shouting the second I tried to focus. That’s my ADHD. It’s 22 songs playing at the same time while my internal narration screams in the corner about the Amazon returns I forgot, the chores I didn’t do, and the doctor’s appointment I still haven’t made.

But then there’s the Autistic side. That’s the part of me standing in the middle of all that noise, desperately craving a silent, rigid, perfectly organized room where nothing ever changes. It’s the part that forces me into the "replay booth," obsessively dissecting every social interaction from three hours—or three years—ago, analyzing every word I said and every face I made to see where I "failed" to be normal.

I spent decades wondering why everyone else received the instruction manual to life while I was just handed a bunch of glitter in a pasta strainer. The manual was written for people who can hear one voice at a time and move through the world without a script. No wonder the instructions leaked right through the holes; I was too busy trying to keep the glitter from flying away while my brain was busy trying to rebuild the strainer from scratch.

I was 40 years late to my own diagnosis—which, honestly, is the most on-brand ADHD thing I have ever done.

**Welcome to my brain: it has 47 open tabs, 3 are frozen, and I finally know exactly where the music is coming from.**

If you’re here because your own tabs are frozen, welcome. If you’re here because you’re the "ride or die" for someone like me and you’re trying to understand why the music is playing in the first place, welcome. You’re in the right place.

### The Labels & The Sanctuary
Growing up wasn't just "hard"—it was a constant state of walking on pins and needles. In my birth mother’s eyes, I was never the "right" kind of daughter. I was the kid who was too much and not enough all at once. The labels were thrown at me like darts: *Lazy. Unmotivated. Dramatic. Intense. Defiant. Bad.* All of them echoed in my head, but the one that rang the loudest—the one that became a 40-year curse—was her telling me I was going to be **nothing.**

My only escape was my grandparents' house. That was my sanctuary. Grandma and Grandpa were the first ones at every school event. They were the only ones who didn't yell when I asked "why" for the tenth time. At their house, I could just *exist*. I could engage in parallel play—just being in the room, doing my own thing—and finally feel regulated.

### The Spy in the Mirror
But as soon as that car turned back into my driveway, the mask went right back on. And holy shit, did I get good at masking.

I spent hours in the bathroom mirror practicing my facial expressions. I wasn't just "getting ready"; I was scripting. I was studying the "normals" like a spy trying to learn a foreign language just to survive. I had to hide the overstimulation and the burnout that was constantly clawing at my insides.

On top of my own internal chaos, I was being trained to manage the chaos of others. My mother taught me how to lie to my father to cover for her—hiding bills or pretending she’d already paid the electric company when they showed up to shut the power off. 

**I HATED lying.** I still do. Every fiber of my being felt the wrongness of it, but I learned that lying was the only thing that would save me from the next fight. It was survival. It was masking an ADHD or Autistic moment, hiding a mistake, or suppressing a stim because I was living with **Rejection Sensitive Dysphoria (RSD)** before I even knew the word for it. If I forgot my homework, I wasn't just "forgetful"—I was paralyzed by the fear of letting everyone down and the hell-storm that followed. 

### The Mirror & The Moment of Truth
That experience followed me into adulthood. I ended up in toxic relationships that were mirrors of my childhood. I saw the patterns in their behaviors and would exhaust every fucking ounce of my energy trying to manage *their* emotions just to avoid the next explosion. I was told I was the problem. I was told I wasn't good enough. 

Then, my kids were born. 

When my oldest two were diagnosed with ADHD, I saw the patterns. I thought, *'Okay, that fits me, but there’s still something missing.'* Then came my youngest, Jackson. **Autistic.**

It was a holy shit moment. Everything I had been through—the "too much" energy, the need for sameness, the sensory pain, the "nothing" labels—it all snapped into focus. I wasn't a boiling pot of disaster. I was **AuDHD**. 

The noise of the past 40 years is still loud, and the Imposter Syndrome is a bitch, but for the first time, I’m not apologizing for the spicy way my brain is wired.
